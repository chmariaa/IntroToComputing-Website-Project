The Painstaking Process of Bringing The Little Prince to Life

When director Mark Osborne was asked to adapt The Little Prince, Antoine de Saint-Exupéry's beloved 1943 children's book, into an animated film, he hesitated. “Turning this precious book into a big movie would just break it,” said Osborne, a two-time Oscar nominee. “The Little Prince feels very lyrical, poetic. It's not movie-shaped at all.” The cherished story, about the Little Prince's quest to return home, is rich with philosophy on love and loss and enhanced by Saint-Exupéry's whimsical illustrations. To preserve the essence of the book, Osborne decided to use computer-generated animation for a larger framing narrative about a lonely girl, then shift to stop-motion animation to tell the Little Prince's story, which the girl, known only as Little Girl, learns about from an eccentric old neighbor voiced by Jeff Bridges.

“When you're reading the book, you're told over and over again in the text, 'These drawings aren't very good,' and you're actually being tricked into imagining the reality Saint-Exupéry was trying to depict,” says Osborne “There's a charm to those illustrations, and we wanted our stop-motion animation to be charming like they are: handcrafted, obviously the work of artists.” Stop-motion production designer Alexander Juhasz, who previously worked on the eerie children's book in *The Babadook*, led a team of artists to sculpt the tiny characters, all dressed in hand-painted rice-paper costumes, while staying true to Saint-Exupéry's illustrations. In the captions for these photos, Juhasz explains how he combined his own vision of *The Little Prince* with Saint-Exupéry's. “When you're interpreting someone else's designs into your own work,” Juhasz says, “you need to know, 'This is home base.' I want our film to feel like, 'This is what Saint-Exupéry captured in his writing.’”

The kind Aviator, shown in the sketches at right, is never depicted in Saint-Exupéry's book, so Juhasz took time to “nail him down.” He explains, “I wanted him to be this sweet, compelling, relatable guy — it's about his vulnerability and getting at something empathetic.”

“'It is only with the heart that one can see rightly,’” says Juhasz, quoting the book's Fox character. “He's wise, he's elusive. He should feel like a watercolor brushstroke that just dried.” To bring this to life, the team used hand-painted fur and a paper tail. “He came to life as this little painting.”

Juhasz notes, “Saint-Exupéry would draw empty circles for eyes, so we thought, Let's roll with it — keep things as simple as possible and give the Little Prince an extremely limited expression set.”

In the book, “he looks a little wonky,” Juhasz explains. “He has a long oval head, and his hairline sometimes starts at the back of his neck. He looks balding. We had to get to a point where he's cute.”

“He's a very somber, pensive young man,” Juhasz says of the Little Prince. “So we thought, 'Let's not make him a caricature.’” While sculpting him, “I tried to think, What would a line drawing look like? His expressions are very neutral, even his smile.”

In Osborne's storyboards, the Businessman whom the Little Prince meets “was this larger-than-life, huge figure on his planet,” Juhasz explains. “He's like this mountain; he resembles the mountain of paperwork he has. When I designed him, I wanted him to be almost like a turtle coming out of its shell. He's been sitting at this desk so long, he's, like, part of the desk.”

Juhasz struggled with creating the Little Prince's beloved Rose, voiced by Marion Cotillard in the film. “With a lot of the initial test roses, we'd say, 'Oh, wow, that looks nice,' and then the next day go, 'Oh, well, this just looks like a paper flower.’” To capture the Rose's unique beauty, Juhasz asked stop-motion art director Corinne Merrell (whose hand is seen here) to create something “flatter, more graphic, more interesting. It was a matter of avoiding a cliché paper flower.”

This image shows the Little Prince and his treasured rose in a scene from the finished film. “People have strong feelings for *The Little Prince*,” Juhasz says. “Our work was so much about protecting those feelings.”
