Antoine de Saint-Exupéry's Original Watercolors for “The Little Prince”

In April 1943, shortly after The Little Prince was published, 43-year-old Saint-Exupéry handed his friend Silvia Hamilton a brown paper bag containing his manuscript and drawings, saying, “I'd like to give you something splendid, but this is all I have.” He then left for Algiers to serve as a military pilot with the Free French Air Force. Although he was eight years beyond the age limit for pilots in these squadrons, he persistently petitioned for an exemption, which was ultimately granted by General Dwight Eisenhower. On July 31, 1944, he departed on a reconnaissance mission and was never seen again. He was 44 years old at the time of his death — a detail that adds a poignant layer to the fact that the Little Prince watched the sunset 44 times while on his little planet.

Years later, parts of Saint-Exupéry's plane were discovered. A fisherman near Marseilles found the author's silver bracelet in his net. The inscription on it included Saint-Exupéry's name, his wife's name, and the address of his American publisher, with "NYC USA" written as well.

In 1968, The Morgan Library in New York, home to valuable treasures like rare Islamic paintings depicting the life of Rumi and illustrated to-do lists by famous artists, acquired the original manuscripts of Saint-Exupéry. A new exhibition at the Morgan delves into his creative process, showcasing writings he excluded from the final version. The manuscript, containing 30,000 words—almost double the length of the published book—along with his little-known original watercolors and other biographical items, offers a deeper insight. Saint-Exupéry was a unique artist, one of the few writers who also illustrated his own works, like other renowned creators such as Maurice Sendak, J.R.R. Tolkien, and Maira Kalman, where text and images work together to enrich one another.

What makes these drawings so special is that they both reflect and challenge Saint-Exupéry's famous line — "What is essential is invisible to the eye." The drawings try to make visible the creative effort of a man whose story continues to touch generations with its gentle message about the meaning of life.

Saint-Exupéry, like many famous writers, had unusual habits and questionable sleep routines. He often wrote late into the night and had no problem calling a friend at 2 A.M. to read a passage aloud. He always carried a cup of coffee or tea and usually had a cigarette in his mouth. Unsurprisingly, these habits left their mark on his work, as seen in the coffee stain on an early manuscript page of The Little Prince.

When I asked Saint-Exupéry how *The Little Prince* came into his life, he told me that one day he looked at what seemed like a blank sheet of paper and saw a small, childlike figure. “I asked him who he was,” Saint-Exupéry said. “I'm the Little Prince,” was the reply.

Saint-Exupéry's beloved character developed mostly in the margins of his letters and notebooks. In a 1940 letter to a friend, he sketched a character with thinning hair, similar to his own, wearing a bow-tie. He made a similar drawing for Elizabeth Reynal, a French-speaking friend in New York, who later encouraged him to turn this character into a children's story.

One of the most beautiful pieces in the exhibit is a 1943 review of The Little Prince by P.L. Travers for the New York Herald Tribune. It echoes Tolkien's belief that there is no such thing as writing "for children" and expresses the book's profound, timeless appeal with great sensitivity.
